Introduction

A Living
Freshwater World

The pond ecosystem is a freshwater environment that can reveal the health of a local area. Like a mirror of nature, the pond ecosystem has specific life forms that show its overall health. Toxins or pollution can affect the pond ecosystem adversely.


The pond ecosystem begins with what lives in water — from the smallest microbes, single-cell creatures, to the guppies, teachers and midges. Only clean water can sustain life. The plants that convert oxygen from these creatures are an important part of the fauna. A healthy pond ecosystem will have a balance of both plant and animal life living within its parameters.

⚖️
Balance
A healthy pond maintains equilibrium between producers, consumers, and decomposers in a continuous cycle.
🔬
Indicators
Pond species act as bioindicators — their presence or absence tells us about environmental quality.
🌊
Cascade Effect
Loss of one species triggers ripple effects through the food chain, threatening the entire ecosystem.
Observations

Abiotic
Components

Abiotic factors are the non-living factors that can have an impact on the ecosystem. These shape the physical environment in which all living organisms must survive.

💧
Water Quality
The quantity of water is crucial for living organisms in the pond. Only clean water can sustain life in the ecosystem.
🌡️
Temperature
Extremes impact oxygen levels. Too hot — fish struggle to breathe. Too cold — ice forms, blocking sunlight and O₂.
☀️
Light
Plants need sunlight for photosynthesis, producing oxygen not just above but below the water surface for aquatic life.
🧪
pH Level
Too low or too high acidity in the water can clog a fish's gills and make reproduction more challenging.
🌱
Soil
The soil needs to contain enough moisture to keep surrounding plants alive. Moist, fertile soil helps plants thrive.
🍂
Seasonal Change
Spring and fall are the seasons that keep the ecosystem healthy and minimize negative effects on organisms.
Biotic Factors

The Living
Community

Biotic factors relate to all the living things in the ecosystem. Their presence and biological by-products affect the composition of an ecosystem — from animals and humans, to plants, fungi and bacteria.

♻️
Decomposers — Nature's Recyclers
Animal waste, dead and decaying plants, and animal remains form detritus on the bottom of the pond. Decomposers — also known as detritivores — are bacteria and other organisms that break down detritus into material that can be used by primary producers. During decay, microbes living on detritus pull nutrients from the overlying water, improving water quality. In the process of breaking down detritus, decomposers produce water and carbon dioxide.

Conclusion

What We
Discovered

Ponds are greatly influenced by humans, even though we don't pay much attention to them — they seem very man-made and controlled with man-made water falls or species not native like koi. We found out that there are many paths to a pond, like niches, predators, prey, toxic waste, and more — and they can all be affected by us taking action.
